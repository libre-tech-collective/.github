Revised 19-JUL-2026


## What we can build and offer

**In brief**, what I see as viable in this model centers around meeting users where their needs are, now, and growing with them as their needs evolve into the future. This begins with eliminating the biggest present "pain point" on both sides of this relationship (artificial intelligence), and expands to slowly liberate users from the bondage of mainstream technology. Eventually, we should be less a "separate entity" from our users/clients, and more a cooperative liaison, providing:

1. **Automation solutions for manual and repetitive admin tasks.** This is a "bread and butter" service that all users -- from individuals to NPOs and small businesses -- can benefit from. It enables a build-once-implement-many model that can solve a wide array of problems in a cost-effective way for clients, in a time-efficient way for us, in a subscription-style service that ensures a steady source of income for workers and a guaranteed level of sustainability for clients.
2. **Support and maintenance of tech infrastructure.** Automation is only an entryway or "landing pad" for clients to see an immediate return on their investment. We don't stop there. Once we solve their immediate problems with automations where they can be implemented, we probe their operations model. We're not trying to sell them new tech. We want to maximize what they already have, so that we only recommend new investments when essential. We look for places where open-source solutions can better serve their needs and eliminate the cost of proprietary licensing and subscriptions. *We build solutions that meet users where they are, instead of asking them to accommodate a learning curve or change their way of doing things.* This builds trust and gives us a steady stream of learning opportunities to enhance our understanding of our clients, which we can reinvest in future development.
3. **Compliance and security services.** Nobody is immune to information security and data integrity risk, and most businesses have some kind of compliance standard they strive for (for example, any business that processes credit cards should at least try to abide by the PCI-DSS, even if they aren't seeking certification). This is often out of reach for small businesses and non-profits that cannot afford to hire a consultant or staff a CySec professional. Instead, they are force to rely on built-in security of services and software they use, which tends to be difficult to understand and comes with layers of legal jargon that protect the provider in cases where security fails; it encourages a pattern of prioritizing apologies and blame-shifting when breaches *do* occur, instead of proactive prevention to reduce them in the first place. Our clients and their customers deserve better than that. Let us be the crew that provides real security and data integrity solutions, instead of just excuses and aversion of responsibility.
4. **Education.** I already mentioned that this is a two-way street. We must endeavor to meet our users where they are. But they aren't stupid, and we want them to know that we recognize their ability to learn and grow. *For those who are willing and interested,* we should make it a goal to turn users into power users. This is untenable for a consultant who has to charge an hourly rate to meet their own needs, but in a subscription-based model, we can afford to provide education to users when they want it, and help them bridge the knowledge gap. This is how we teach them to be smart users, avoid the pitfalls of social media, navigate open-source documentation, understand how to solve basic problems, ..., the list goes on. In particular, we want to focus on teaching them to favor efficiency, functionality, and security over aesthetics, branding, and consumerism.

### A few real-world examples

One of the biggest complaints, aside from cost, that I have heard from users of mainstream technology solutions over the years is that they persistently fall short of meeting real needs, while their platforms grow exponentially with "garbage features." This represents a very real counter-intuitivity problem in mainstream, proprietary technology, especially in the software world. Each new version of a software introduces dozens of new features and functionality, but none of them offer solutions to the real problems users face every day. Instead, they pile on new ways of doing things that users only recently found "a groove" for in the previous iteration, while their real struggles go unacknowledged.

If you were around for the "Windows 8" era, you may know exactly what I'm talking about. When Microsoft released Windows 8, it introduced a brand new UI, meant to force users to transition to touch-screen type interfaces and tablets. Users never asked for this; they were quite content with the successful Windows 7 UI/UX, and many of them had only recently gotten comfortable on that platform, only to be uprooted and thrown into a new environment that they couldn't navigate. I was a sysadmin at the time, and my users absolutely hated the change. In the first week of he roll-out, I spent so much time teaching users how to turn off the tablet-oriented interface that I ended up scripting a GPO to do it after three days.

This is not ideal.

## On Organizational Structure

> [!NOTE]  
> For full transparency, I am **not** a business-oriented person. I don't know enough about business to make this a reality in the world as it is. I *do* know tech, and I can provide the ideas all day long. But I need help to realize them.  
>  
> Fortunately, I have friends in the [IWW](https://www.iww.org) who know the "lay of the land" much better than I do. I would strongly encourage all workers, regardless of your position, to join.  
>  
> My vision is primarily driven by the ideas of anarcho-syndicalism, and I hoped to use existing technologies (i.e., Web3 decentralized, anonymized networks; open-source development and evolution) to facilitate non-hierarchical and non-capitalist models for managing the "means of production" in the technology industry in a democratized, worker-owned model that upholds the principles of anarcho-syndicalism for a new generation.  
>
> In this context, "democratized" deserves some clarification. Why not just call it a "co-op"? Primarily because I'm not well versed enough in the semantics of modern business to use that term with certainty. But also because, like many of my libertarian (in the traditional sense; not in the Americanized sense) brethren, I am dubious of the vague term "democracy." When it implies a direct and participatory democratic structure, that is truly an ideal to strive for. But, too often, it is a veiled implication for a system of "representation" that limits the influence of the individual and places the majority of decision-making power in the hands of the "representatives;" this is not ideal.  
>  
> So I have used the term "syndicate" in recent talks, as I'm more comfortable with what that term means and, as of yet, it has not been tainted by the influence of semantic manipulation or coopted by the less liberation-oriented left (i.e., Marxist-Leninists and state socialists masquerading as "communists"), nor the "anarcho-capitalists" in the Libertarian Party of the US.  
>  
> **The goal here is nothing short of a worker-owned, direct-and-participatory democratic structure that targets the technology industry at large by starting with a single, localized collective.** If we can facilitate that and ensure its longevity through the use an ownerless (or "headless") organization, like a DAO, then all the better; if not, as long as we can ensure an egalitarian (and borderline communalist) organization, the goal has been met. Regardless, none of this should be understood and requiring a "buy in" from members; membership should be free and open to any who want to participate, in the interest of making enterprise-level technology solutions available to non-profits, small businesses, and individuals who cannot afford enterprise-level solutions in the capitalist mainstream.  
>  
> If someone who is more versed than I am in the "business" side of this, and knows how to make this a reality, I'll give you the framework to do it, as long as it remains free, open, and collectively managed.
