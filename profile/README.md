# LIBRE: A Tech Collective

The world of technology is changing!

Well, okay. The world of tech is *always* changing. But the rate of change is accelerating and the impacts of those changes are beginning to ripple out, further and further, year after year. Those impacts are hitting closer to home than ever before for IT workers and that's not necessarily a good thing.

A not-so-insignificant handful of us are finding our day jobs being replaced by generative AI, and that's a bigger problem than many people realize. Especially those people who are enjoying the benefits of having access to AI that, for all intents and purposes, seems to "replace" their tech staff as far as they can tell. What they view as an inconsequential convenience has significant upstream impacts that threaten far more than the average tech worker's role in businesses around the world.

As the bosses and bankers of the world turn to AI, tech industry workers must adapt and reposition themselves if we want to stay relevant. Artificial intelligence looks good on paper, and in early stages and demos, it is immensely alluring (particularly to smaller businesses that lack the spending power for human staff). But it is not without its drawbacks:

- The "low cost lie" is already making itself apparent as early adopters of AI are finding their investments don't actually save them money
- The environmental impact of AI is immense, threatening multiple vectors of environmental sustainability
- The resource consumption of AI (particularly water) presents an existential threat to humanity at large
- The ability for AI to "get it right" often enough, rather than falling into its own poor logic, fact hallucinations, and down-right incorrect assumptions is already drawing scrutiny
- The proven potential for an AI to "go rogue" introduces a new level of uncertainty and concern that technology has never presented in the past

All of these concerns provide fertile ground for tech workers to provide better, more reliable, and more sustainable alternatives to artifical intelligence. By prioritizing cost-effectiveness and quality, we have a ready-made base clientele in local and small businesses that cannot afford (or simply lack interest in) the gamble on AI. But how? We still have to make a living; we can't afford to work for free.

There is a solution, somewhere, if we're willing to put in some effort up front. Automation technology (particularly with open-source platforms like [n8n](https://www.n8n.io)) is a very realistic middle ground for most use-cases where AI might find footing -- and it still requires a skilled person to setup and maintain, if only for a few hours each month. Additionally, open source technology has come a long way in the last few decades; it provides viable alternatives to mainstream solutions, albeit with a bit of a learning curve; closing that knowledge gap and empowering end users with enough know-how to get through their day-to-day could become a substantial niche with the right outreach. Open source software carries with it a long history of democratization and communalism: what if we could extend this to providing on-demand support for technology solutions that seek to add qualitative value to clients, and empower them with the knowledge to close gaps of understanding in the process? And what if we could make that our "day job" -- not to get rich, but to get by?

## A Vision

Imagine a platform that users could pay a monthly subscription for and get help building automations to meet their needs, and education to learn their technology well enough to use it, rather than be used by it. Eventually, a network of open-source specialists all over the world who put in the time whenever they have it to help others -- whether that's a busy small business owner looking for a payroll automation who just doesn't have time to learn to do it, or a trade worker who wants to learn to code so they can build their own utilities to make their job easier.

And what if everyone paid in a little -- some in time, some in money, as their means and needs dictate -- to keep this service available. The more people who pay in in money, the more time those paying in time can give. All share the load, and all benefit. It's much less expensive for those who aren't tech inclined than hiring IT staff, and much more lucrative than the 9-to-5 for IT workers. If 250 small businesses pay $500 each, per month, for unlimited access, and 10 tech workers are supporting them, each of those tech workers earns about $150,000. Perhaps the collective holds 20% of that to ensure it can cover it's operating costs -- and reinvest in additional offerings, like managed cloud services and filling needs for software solutions that business actually need, instead of some cooking-cutter "feature" that just bogs down users.

This also provides a new level of guarnateed security and stability. When the goal of the technology producer stops being profit, and is allowed to refocus on innovation and quality, something miraculous happens: people start to trust technology again. Eventually, they begin to trust each other.

This isn't just about preserving tech workers' sustainability in troubling times. It is about providing a model that can be replicated in other industries and professional domains so that they may preempt their own obsolescence; a model for solidarity and collectivism in a globally-connected but individualism-oriented world.
